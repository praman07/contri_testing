# User Experience

Using Override should feel like working with a quiet Chief Engineer who understands the workspace, remembers history, and intervenes only when useful.

## First boot

Override introduces what it can observe, what it can remember, and what it can control. It requests permissions gradually and explains consequences. It creates a local profile and asks which actions require explicit approval.

## Returning after a week

Override summarizes active projects, unresolved tasks, recent changes, open goals, and suggested next steps. It distinguishes facts from guesses.

## Coding

Override identifies the repository, branch, changed files, failing checks, running services, and relevant documentation. It proposes a plan before edits, uses providers to act, runs checks, and reports evidence.

## Studying and learning

Override tracks learning goals, materials, weak areas, sessions, and progress. It recommends practice when relevant and remains respectful when the user chooses something else.

## Meetings

With permission, Override prepares context, captures notes, extracts decisions, tracks action items, and reconnects outcomes to projects and goals.

## Research

Override builds a source-backed workspace: key claims, citations, contradictions, unknowns, diagrams, and final conclusions.

## Job applications

Override asks for resume data, finds roles, maps requirements to experience, tailors materials, handles forms with approval, and tracks status. It never lies, fabricates, or submits without permission.

## Switching projects

Override recognizes a context switch, preserves the previous session state, loads the new project model, and offers a concise handoff summary.

## Long-running goals

Override connects daily behavior to goals only when helpful. It may say, “This looks unrelated to your active FDE goal; should I pause that goal or keep it active?”
